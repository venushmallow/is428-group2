Local Dengue Outbreak Analysis

Local Dengue outbreak analysis on Climate, Standard of Living, Geographical Location, and the implications of mitigation efforts such as Project Wolbachia.

The local analysis is focused on data from 2015 - 2022. While we do have more historical data on weather conditions, limited data on dengue cases and the distribution of cases in Singapore restricted our time frame to 2015 - 2022 to standardise our analysis.

Dengue Cases Throughout The Years in Singapore


Based on the animated map, we are not able to observe any obvious patterns. However, the line chart depicting the dengue cases across time has some interesting trends that are worth noting. Firstly, there are regular peaks and troughs in the trend which may suggest seasonality. Secondly, we observe that there is a huge dip in dengue cases in 2017 and 2018. Our initial hypothesis is that climate factors are the cause of the seasonality in the trend. To test our hypothesis, we started off with a line graph to observe the relationship between both surface temperature and rainfall between 2015 to 2020. To better visualise the potential seasonality, we also grouped the line graph by the months they fall in.


Line Chart of Singapore Surface Temperature Through Time 2015 - 2020


The line graph shows a peak in dengue cases in August while the mean surface temperature peaks in May. There appears to be some lag time between the peaks in surface temperature and dengue cases. This led us to do further research and we came across a research piece that may hold the explanation for this delay. The study highlighted that there is a 90 days delay between peak Aedes mosquito population and the peak in the dengue epidemic [16]. Our updated hypothesis is that the hotter months of March, April and May results in higher Aedes population which then contributes to higher dengue cases in July, August and September due to the 90 day delay.


Line Chart of Singapore Rainfall Through Time 2015 - 2020

The line graph seems to suggest a negative correlation between rainfall and dengue cases. December is the peak rainfall season while it has the least dengue cases. On the other hand, August has relatively less rainfall but is the peak period for dengue transmission. This left us puzzled as all the research we have done thus far suggests that rainfall is key in the propagation of the Aedes mosquito. In fact, one of the research papers we researched suggested that rainfall days in the previous month is a good indicator for dengue cases [17]. Hence, to check our hypotheses, we created a scatterplot matrix to check for correlation between each climate factor and the number of dengue cases. The scatterplot matrix is shown in the next section.


Scatterplot Matrix of Weather Factors in relation to Dengue Cases in Singapore

We found that with the exception of Total Rainfall, all other metrics had significant but weak correlation with dengue cases. Temperature, represented by mean temperature and wet bulb temperature, had a positive correlation with dengue cases which supports our hypothesis. However, for the number of rainy days it has a negative correlation with the number of cases. This seems to suggest that changes in rainfall in Singapore has no effect on the spread of dengue. This could be because of the fact that Singapore is a tropical country and even when it doesn’t rain as much, there is still more than enough stale water around for the propagation of the Aedes mosquito.


Choropleth Map to View The Population Density at Different Subzones and Planning Areas


From the choropleth map we can see that the East, particularly at Tampines region, has the highest number of dwelling units as represented by the dark blue colour. After adding the dengue cases layer, we can see that the areas with the greatest number of dengue cases are Aljunied, Woodlands east, Choa Chu Kang (Peng Siang region) and Jurong West (Yunnan) as represented by the dark red colour on the density map.

Choropleth Map to View The Population Density AND Number of Water Bodies at Different Subzones and Planning Areas


Based on the choropleth map, we notice that Jurong West (West), Woodlands (North), Seng Kang (North East) and Bukit Merah (South) are areas that are most densely populated. The areas with the most dengue cases are Geylang (South East), Bedok (South East), Woodlands (North) and Jurong West (West). At first glance, Geylang and Bedok appear to contradict our hypothesis that more densely populated areas are more susceptible to dengue as they are both not densely populated. However, Geylang is surrounded by the Geylang River and Kallang River, while Bedok is split in half by the Siglap Canal. Through this, we see the combined effect of both proximity to water bodies and population density on dengue cases.

Dashboard of Cluster of Dengue Cases and Residential Dwellings in Singapore


The scatter plot which compares the number of dengue cases and total number of dwelling units shows that there is no very clear trend but a very slight positive correlation can be seen between the two variables. Many of the points still cluster at the lower end of the y-axis even though the value on the x-axis increases. This indicates that as the number of dwelling units increases, the number of dengue cases in that area increases very slightly. Additionally from the stacked barchart, we can see that the type of dwelling has no direct relationship to the number of dengue cases at those subzones. Outliers can also be seen where places with a low number of dwelling units have a high number of dengue cases such as in Seletar Hills and Aljunied. The other kind of outlier is where there is a high number of dwelling units but lower than expected number of dengue cases for example at Tampines East and Bedok North.

Line Chart and Chloropleth Map of Dengue Cases After and Before Project Wolbachia


Project Wolbachia aims to reduce mosquito-borne disease transmission by suppressing mosquito populations. When Wolbachia males mate with dengue carrying females, the eggs produced will not hatch. Hence NEA is trying this method in an attempt to reduced mosquito populations. Phase 1 was conducted In Tampines and Yishun, where male Wolbachia-Aedes mosquitoes have been released for more than a year. NEA observed up to 98% reduction in the dengue mosquito population and up to 88% drop in dengue cases.



The animation shown illustrates the dengue cases across Tampines and Yishun from October 2015 to November 2020. This helps us see and gauge visually that there was indeed a drop in cases after the implementation of Project Wolbachia from October 2016. At the beginning of this animation, big red dots are observed, indicating a high number of cases in October 2015. The red dots gradually reduced in size after October 2017.

Phase 1 of Project Wolbachia started in Oct 2016 till Jan 2017. We observe that dengue cases have generally decreased after the implementation of this project. While we see a small increase in cases for Tampines before Feb 2017, this could be due to the fact that it typically takes at least three to four months before there is a significant reduction of Aedes population according to the NEA. However, from 2019 onwards, cases have increased again. Reports have shown that it is due to the higher temperatures observed in 2019 and 2020 in Singapore. Also, dengue transmission was more prevalent in 2020 due to more people working from home as part of the COVID-19 safety measures.

Overall, the project is still ongoing up till today, we can conclude that for this project to be effective, it has to ramp up rapidly to beat the effects of climate changes.
