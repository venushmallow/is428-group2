Local Dengue Outbreak Analysis

Local Dengue outbreak analysis on Climate, Standard of Living, Geographical Location, and the implications of mitigation efforts such as Project Wolbachia.

The local analysis is focused on data from 2015 - 2022. While we do have more historical data on weather conditions, limited data on dengue cases and the distribution of cases in Singapore restricted our time frame to 2015 - 2022 to standardise our analysis.

Dengue Cases Throughout The Years in Singapore


There appears to be a steady upward trend from 2015 to 2020 except for the significant drop in dengue cases in 2017 and 2018. When looking further into the months, month of July to September have a spike in cases.


Line Chart of Singapore Surface Temperature Through Time 2015 - 2020


The peak in dengue cases (July August, September) seem to follow a peak in surface temperature (March, April, May). A possible explanation for this delay is that there is lag time between rise in temperature, followed by the rise in aedes population then the rise in the spread of dengue cases.


Scatterplot Matrix of Weather Factors in relation to Dengue Cases in Singapore

There is a weak negative correlation for number of rainy days, rainfall and humidity with the number of cases. There is a weak positive correlation for mean temperature and wet bulb temperature with the number of cases.


Choropleth Map to View The Population Density at Different Subzones and Planning Areas


From the choropleth map we can see that the East, particularly at Tampines region, has the highest number of dwelling units as represented by the dark blue colour. After adding the dengue cases layer, we can see that the areas with the greatest number of dengue cases are Aljunied, Woodlands east, Choa Chu Kang (Peng Siang region) and Jurong West (Yunnan) as represented by the dark red colour on the density map.

Choropleth Map to View The Number of Water Bodies at Different Subzones and Planning Areas


For areas like Geylang and Bedok, although they are not densely populated, they are close to water bodies.

Choropleth Map to View The Population Density AND Number of Water Bodies at Different Subzones and Planning Areas


The most densely populated areas represented by the darkest shade of green include: Jurong West, Woodlands, Seng Kang and Bukit Merah. The areas with the most dengue cases represented by the red density plots are: Jurong West, Woodlands, Geylang and Bedok. The two anomalies are Geylang and Bedok, as they are both areas with many cases but not densely populated. However these areas are both close to water bodies, suggesting that both proximity to water bodies and population density does indeed affect dengue cases.

Dashboard of Cluster of Dengue Cases and Residential Dwellings in Singapore


The scatter plot which compares the number of dengue cases and total number of dwelling units shows that there is no very clear trend but a very slight positive correlation can be seen between the two variables. Many of the points still cluster at the lower end of the y-axis even though the value on the x-axis increases. This indicates that as the number of dwelling units increases, the number of dengue cases in that area increases very slightly. Additionally from the stacked barchart, we can see that the type of dwelling has no direct relationship to the number of dengue cases at those subzones. Outliers can also be seen where places with a low number of dwelling units have a high number of dengue cases such as in Seletar Hills and Aljunied. The other kind of outlier is where there is a high number of dwelling units but lower than expected number of dengue cases for example at Tampines East and Bedok North.

Line Chart and Chloropleth Map of Dengue Cases After and Before Project Wolbachia


Project Wolbachia aims to reduce mosquito-borne disease transmission by suppressing mosquito populations. When Wolbachia males mate with dengue carrying females, the eggs produced will not hatch. Hence NEA is trying this method in an attempt to reduced mosquito populations. Phase 1 was conducted In Tampines and Yishun, where male Wolbachia-Aedes mosquitoes have been released for more than a year. NEA observed up to 98% reduction in the dengue mosquito population and up to 88% drop in dengue cases.



we observe that dengue cases have generally decreased after the implementation of this project. While we see a small increase in cases for Tampines before Feb 2017. This could be due to the fact that it typically takes at least three to four months before there is a significant reduction of Aedes population according to the NEA.
